Section 1

Why most sustainable IT programmes fail.

Most failures are not technical gaps. They are strategic gaps. The five most common failure modes are avoidable with deliberate programme design.

Failure mode 1

Unclear ownership

The programme belongs to everyone collectively — which means it belongs to no one specifically. When budget decisions, leadership changes, or operational pressure arrive, there is no named owner to hold the line.

Failure mode 2

Undefined priorities

Without a materiality assessment, every sustainability issue appears equally important. Organisations that try to address everything simultaneously deliver nothing well.

Failure mode 3

No connection to corporate commitments

A sustainable IT programme that exists in isolation from corporate sustainability goals will compete for investment as if it were a discretionary initiative — and will usually lose.

Failure mode 4

No multi-year roadmap

Organisations that try to implement measurement systems, governance frameworks, procurement reforms, and operational improvements simultaneously typically deliver none of them well.

Failure mode 5

Strategy without governance

A strategy document that does not specify who owns decisions, what forums review progress, and what KPIs are reviewed on what cadence will produce a report rather than a programme.

Section 2

A strategy document has five components — all five must be present.

A document that contains only aspiration and priorities without governance and investment is a narrative, not a strategy. A governance instrument has all five.

1
Current state assessment

Where you are today — with evidence. Baseline footprint, measurement coverage, governance gaps, supplier evidence quality, operational maturity. The honest starting point, not the aspirational one.

2
Ambition and targets

Where you are going and by when — connected to corporate commitments. SBTi-aligned intensity targets, absolute reduction trajectories, and measurement coverage improvements. Targets must be achievable and verifiable.

3
Strategic priorities

The areas of highest material importance — identified through materiality assessment. In order of priority, with an explanation of why these and not everything else. Sequenced, not simultaneous.

4
Governance model

Named owners for each domain. Decision forums and their cadence. KPIs reviewed on what schedule. Escalation paths. Without this, the strategy has no operating mechanism.

5
Investment case

What funding is required, in what phases, with what expected return. Including the Phase 1 self-funding argument if applicable. Without this, the strategy cannot be approved.

Section 3

OKRs force the specificity that sustainability programmes often lack.

You cannot write a meaningful OKR without being clear about what you're trying to achieve and how you'll know you've achieved it. That discipline is exactly what most sustainable IT programmes are missing.

OKR cadence

Quarterly check-ins in existing governance forums — not separate sustainability meetings. Green/amber/red status for each key result. This is how sustainability becomes part of operational rhythm rather than a parallel track.

OKR Example 1 — Emissions reduction
Objective: Reduce the carbon intensity of the technology estate by 30% within three years, aligned to the corporate SBTi pathway.
KR1 Baseline documented and published by Q1 — confidence levels stated, gaps explicit
KR2 Top-10 service register with named owners and intensity metrics live by Q2
KR3 10% reduction in carbon per unit across top-10 services by year-end
OKR Example 2 — Maturity progression
Objective: Achieve Level 4 maturity on the sustainable IT framework by end of next financial year.
KR1 Governance roles and decision forums defined and operating by Q1
KR2 Vendor evidence register established with top-10 suppliers assessed by Q2
KR3 Role-based KPIs in use and reviewed in operational forums by Q3
OKR Example 3 — Scope 3 credibility
Objective: Demonstrate credible Scope 3 coverage for the top ten technology suppliers.
KR1 Evidence register complete for top-10 suppliers by Q1 — tier level recorded for each
KR2 Missing data requests sent to all suppliers below Tier 2 by Q1
KR3 Coverage confidence ratings improved for 7 of 10 suppliers by year-end

Section 4

Address the problem/solution tension directly in strategic communication.

IT is both a significant contributor to environmental impact and one of the most powerful tools available for addressing it. Holding both truths simultaneously — without diminishing either — is the foundation of a credible and motivating strategic narrative.

⚡ Part of the problem
Technology organisations that are honest about this contribution are more credible than those who only tell the positive story.
  • ▸Energy consumption: data centres, cloud, end-user devices, and networks at scale
  • ▸Water use: cooling systems in data centres, particularly in water-stressed locations
  • ▸Hardware waste: electronics at end of life, often through insufficiently governed channels
  • ▸Supply chain impacts: rare earth minerals, manufacturing emissions, labour risks
  • ▸Scale of demand: growing faster than efficiency gains in most estates
🌱 Part of the solution
Technology is one of the most powerful tools available for the broader sustainability transition.
  • ▸Optimisation platforms that reduce emissions and waste at scale across industries
  • ▸Smart building and energy management systems cutting heating, cooling, lighting
  • ▸Carbon reporting platforms making footprint visible at operational granularity
  • ▸Product-as-a-Service models creating incentives for durability and resource efficiency
  • ▸Digital logistics, route optimisation, supply chain traceability reducing physical emissions

The framing in practice

Acknowledging the footprint and leading with the solution is the foundation of a credible and motivating narrative. It is more honest than only telling the positive story — and more useful than dwelling on the problem without the solution.

Knowledge Check · Module 11 · Q1

An organisation is developing its sustainable IT strategy. The head of sustainability proposes starting by addressing all five strategic priorities — data centre efficiency, cloud optimisation, hardware lifecycle, software practices, and procurement reform — simultaneously in year one. What is the most significant risk of this approach?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

Sequencing matters. Organisations that attempt to implement measurement systems, governance frameworks, procurement reforms, and operational improvements simultaneously typically deliver none of them well. Each workstream competes for the same limited pool of attention, capability, and budget. When deliverables slip — which they do — the programme loses credibility before it has demonstrated value.

The more effective model: identify the two or three priorities that will create the most credibility and operational momentum in Horizon 1, deliver those demonstrably, and use that momentum to fund and justify Horizon 2. This is not ambition reduction — it is sequencing for success.

Knowledge Check · Module 11 · Q2

A sustainable IT strategy document contains a strong current state assessment, clear ambition and targets, and a detailed governance model. What is the most significant gap?

Explanation — correct answer: B

A credible strategy requires all five components: current state assessment, ambition and targets, strategic priorities, governance model, and investment case. This document has three of five. Without strategic priorities, the organisation attempts everything simultaneously and delivers nothing well. Without the investment case, the strategy cannot compete for budget — it is aspiration without a mechanism to fund it. Option A describes a useful communication activity, not a missing strategy component. Option C confuses length with substance.

Key Takeaways

◆
Most programme failures are strategic, not technical.

Unclear ownership, undefined priorities, no corporate connection, and no roadmap. These are the common failure modes — and all are avoidable with deliberate strategy design.

◆
A strategy document has five components — all five must be present.

Current state, ambition, strategic priorities, governance model, and investment case. A document that contains only aspiration without governance is a narrative, not a strategy.

◆
Sequence progress in three horizons.

Horizons 1, 2, and 3 translate from foundations through operational integration to optimisation. Trying to do everything simultaneously typically delivers nothing well.

◆
Strategy without governance produces a report, not a programme.

Named owners, decision forums, KPI review cadence, and escalation paths. These are not administrative details — they are the operating mechanism that makes a strategy function.

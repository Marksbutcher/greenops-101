Section 1

Why most sustainable IT programmes fail.

Most failures are not technical gaps — they are strategic ones. Understanding the five failure modes before building the strategy is the fastest route to avoiding them.

Five strategic failure modes — click any to see what it looks like and how to prevent it
The most common failure mode is not technical

Most sustainable IT programmes fail because they lack named ownership, undefined priorities, no multi-year roadmap, or a strategy document without governance. A strategy that survives scrutiny is specific about ownership, sequenced about priorities, honest about current state, and connected to corporate commitments. Everything else is a narrative document with good intentions.

Section 2

A strategy document has five components — all five must be present.

A document that contains only aspiration and priorities without governance and investment is a narrative, not a strategy. A governance instrument has all five.

1
Current state assessment

Where you are today — with evidence. Baseline footprint, measurement coverage, governance gaps, supplier evidence quality, operational maturity. The honest starting point, not the aspirational one.

2
Ambition and targets

Where you are going and by when — connected to corporate commitments. SBTi-aligned intensity targets, absolute reduction trajectories, and measurement coverage improvements. Targets must be achievable and verifiable.

3
Strategic priorities

The areas of highest material importance — identified through materiality assessment. In order of priority, with an explanation of why these and not everything else. Sequenced, not simultaneous.

4
Governance model

Named owners for each domain. Decision forums and their cadence. KPIs reviewed on what schedule. Escalation paths. Without this, the strategy has no operating mechanism.

5
Investment case

What funding is required, in what phases, with what expected return. Including the Phase 1 self-funding argument if applicable. Without this, the strategy cannot be approved.

Section 3

Three-horizon roadmap — sequence rather than sprint.

The most common roadmap failure is trying to do everything simultaneously. A three-horizon model sequences progress, creates realistic milestones, and gives stakeholders something visible to hold on to across a multi-year programme.

Three-horizon roadmap — click each horizon to see what it contains and how long it takes

Click a horizon to see what it delivers and what it requires.

Organisations that attempt full implementation simultaneously typically deliver none of it well. The programme loses credibility when early deliverables slip — and the whole initiative can collapse. Quick wins in the first 90 days build the credibility that funds Horizon 2.

Quick wins are a strategic priority — not an afterthought

Two or three visible improvements in the first 90 days build the credibility that funds Horizon 2. Lead with waste reduction: it produces cost savings that are easy to defend and demonstrate that the programme produces real operational outcomes rather than reports.

Section 4

OKRs — forcing the specificity that programmes often lack.

OKRs force specificity. You cannot write a meaningful OKR without being clear about what you are trying to achieve and how you will know you have achieved it — which is exactly what sustainable IT programmes often lack. They are a governance tool as much as a measurement one.

Example OKRs — click any objective to expand the key results and success criteria

OKR cadence: quarterly check-ins in existing governance forums — not separate sustainability meetings. Green / amber / red status for each key result. Overdue items escalated by name. If OKRs are reviewed only in sustainability committees, they do not create the broader organisational accountability that sustainable IT requires.

OKRs belong in operational forums — not sustainability committees

If OKRs are reviewed only in sustainability committees, they do not create the broader organisational accountability that sustainable IT requires. Green, amber, red status for each key result, reviewed quarterly in the same forum as cost and performance, is what converts intent into governance.

Module 11 — Key Takeaways

◆
Most programme failures are strategic, not technical.

Unclear ownership, undefined priorities, no connection to corporate goals, and no roadmap. These are the common failure modes — and all are avoidable with deliberate strategy design.

◆
A strategy document has five components — all five must be present.

Current state, ambition, strategic priorities, governance model, and investment case. A document that contains only aspiration and priorities without governance and investment is a narrative, not a strategy.

◆
Sequence progress in three horizons.

Horizons 1, 2, and 3 translate from foundations through operational integration to optimisation. Trying to do everything simultaneously typically delivers nothing well. Start with quick wins and measure them.

◆
OKRs force the specificity that sustainability programmes often lack.

Objectives anchored to corporate targets. Key results that are measurable and named. Quarterly check-ins in existing forums, not separate sustainability meetings. This is what converts strategy into performance management.

◆
Address the problem/solution tension directly in strategic communication.

IT is both a significant contributor and one of the most powerful enablers of the sustainability transition. Acknowledging the footprint honestly is what makes the positive story credible — not something that undermines it.

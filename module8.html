Governance, Maturity
and Making It Stick

Most organisations can write a sustainability strategy. Fewer can sustain one through budget cycles, leadership changes, and the relentless pressure of operational priorities.

Duration26–30 minutes
TrackOperational Practice

Section 1

Role-based accountability.

Governance means role-based accountability, decision forums that matter, measurable KPIs, and evidence that progress is real. Without all four, sustainability becomes a communications function — and the gap between reported and actual position widens every year.

The governance test

If a technology leader cannot speak to the carbon intensity of the top ten services without consulting a sustainability manager, the governance is insufficiently embedded. The test is not whether a report has been published — it is whether named individuals can be held accountable for specific metrics in an operational forum.

Section 2

Greenwashing and greenhushing — both are risks.

Most discussions focus on greenwashing. Greenhushing — staying silent about real sustainability progress out of fear of scrutiny — is an equally significant risk. Neither produces the operational transparency GreenOps requires.

The greenwashing–greenhushing spectrum — click any position to see what it looks like in IT sustainability

Click any position on the spectrum to see what it looks like in practice.

The GreenOps response to greenwashing: make claims specific, measurable, and attributable. The response to greenhushing: communicate progress with evidence, caveats, and confidence levels rather than silence. Investors, auditors, and regulators increasingly see unexplained silence as a risk signal.

Section 3

Maturity is multi-dimensional — assess all five.

A maturity model is most useful when it is multi-dimensional. You can have excellent measurement and weak governance. Strong procurement criteria and almost no operational practice. Mapping all dimensions prevents the illusion of overall maturity from a single strong area.

Most organisations find measurement is furthest ahead, governance and operations lag behind it, and supplier management trails both.

What you actually do: rightsizing, lifecycle extension, dark data remediation, carbon-aware scheduling. The discipline behind the metrics.

Dimension 4

Supplier Management

Evidence quality, assurance tiers, vendor scorecard, circular economy criteria, ITAD governance. Almost always trails the other dimensions.

Dimension 5

Strategy & Innovation

Roadmap maturity, connection to corporate goals, horizon planning, and whether the programme is driving improvement or managing a baseline.

Why multi-dimensional assessment matters

Assessing maturity across five dimensions prevents the illusion of overall maturity from a single strong area. An organisation with excellent measurement but no governance has a metric with no lever. An organisation with strong supplier engagement but no internal data has a story with no foundation. Both dimensions must improve together.

Section 4

Three artefacts that make governance real.

Strategy documents and maturity assessments create a record. These three artefacts create accountability — they force ownership, make progress visible, and produce something that can be held to account in an operational forum.

Knowledge Check · Module 8 · Q1

An organisation uses a maturity model to assess its sustainable IT programme. It scores highly on measurement and data quality, but has not assessed governance, operational practices, supplier management, or innovation. What is the risk of this approach?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

Measurement is almost always the most developed dimension in a sustainability programme — because it was usually the starting point. But strong measurement does not imply strong governance, operational discipline, supplier management, or strategy. An organisation can know exactly what its footprint is and be doing almost nothing to change it.

The multi-dimensional model prevents this illusion. Assessing all five dimensions reveals where genuine progress exists and where significant gaps remain invisible — gaps that would be exploited by any serious external scrutiny.

Knowledge Check · Module 8 · Q2

A CTO is asked by the board to confirm that the organisation's digital sustainability programme is on track. She delegates the response to the sustainability manager. What does this delegation reveal about the governance model?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

Technology leadership owns outcomes, not just targets. A CTO who cannot speak to the carbon intensity of top services, the coverage of the vendor evidence register, and the progress of the waste removal backlog without deferring to the sustainability manager has not embedded the programme into their operating model.

This is a meaningful governance signal — not a minor administrative question. If the CTO doesn't own the answers, it is likely that the programme operates as a parallel function rather than as an integrated discipline within technology governance.

Module 8 — Key Takeaways

◆
Technology leadership owns outcomes, not just targets.

If a CIO or CTO cannot speak to carbon intensity of top services, supplier evidence coverage, and waste removal progress without consulting a sustainability manager, the governance is not yet embedded.

◆
Greenwashing and greenhushing are both risks.

The GreenOps response to both is the same: precision. Explicit confidence levels, clear distinction between activity-based and spend-based data, and honest methodology documentation.

◆
Maturity is multi-dimensional — assess all five.

Measurement is almost always more developed than governance. Supplier management trails both. Mapping all five dimensions prevents the illusion of overall maturity from a single strong area.

◆
Three artefacts convert awareness into accountability.

A top-ten service register, a vendor evidence register, and a waste removal backlog. Without these, the programme will remain honest but ineffective — no named owners, no visible progress, no one held to account.

◆
ITAD is a social governance matter, not only environmental.

Chain-of-custody documentation, data sanitisation to recognised standards, downstream processing commitments, and audit-supporting reporting are the minimum requirements for responsible device disposal.

Procurement, Supply Chains,
and Vendor Evidence

Every purchasing decision is a signal. When you buy without asking for evidence, you signal that evidence is not required. Over time, those signals shape what the market supplies.

Duration24–28 minutes
TrackOperational Practice

What you will take from this module

  • 01Understand the difference between a supplier's corporate ESG commitments and product-level evidence
  • 02Know the seven questions to ask when evaluating a lifecycle assessment or product carbon footprint
  • 03Understand the three meaningful tiers of assurance — and why they are not interchangeable
  • 04Know what good procurement evidence looks like across hardware, data centres, cloud, and SaaS

Section 1

Corporate commitment vs product evidence.

These are entirely different things. Conflating them is one of the most common errors in sustainability procurement. A vendor's net-zero target is a statement about their organisation's future intent. A product carbon footprint is evidence about the specific thing you are buying — now, in the configuration you are buying it.

Corporate commitment
  • →Net-zero targets for the company
  • →Renewable energy pledges
  • →CDP or EcoVadis ratings
  • →Sustainability reports and ESG disclosures
Tells you about the organisation's direction. Does not tell you the carbon footprint of the specific product or service you are procuring.
Product evidence
  • →Product carbon footprint (PCF) for the specific SKU
  • →Lifecycle assessment covering manufacture through disposal
  • →Energy consumption data for the configuration being procured
  • →Third-party assurance with stated standard and scope
Tells you the actual footprint of what you are buying. This is what Scope 3 reporting requires. This is what most vendors do not proactively provide.

Section 2

Three assurance tiers — not interchangeable.

Vendors use the word "verified" loosely. What it actually means depends entirely on what kind of verification was applied. These three tiers represent fundamentally different levels of confidence — treating them as equivalent is a significant data quality error.

Section 3

Building a vendor evidence register.

A vendor evidence register is a living instrument — updated at every contract touchpoint, reviewed at procurement governance forums. Not a spreadsheet filed once a year. It creates a record of supplier engagement that supports both audit and improvement conversations.

Evidence scorecard — use this to rate what each supplier has provided

Questions to ask at every contract renewal: What PCF data is available for this product/service? What assurance tier applies? Has the methodology been stated? Is the evidence specific to the configuration we are procuring? When was the data last updated?

Section 4

Circular procurement and responsible ITAD.

Procurement decisions that consider circularity — repairability, spare parts availability, take-back, and end-of-life treatment — reduce embodied carbon per year of service and reduce e-waste. Responsible ITAD is a governance matter, not only an environmental one: asset disposition involves data security, regulatory compliance, and social supply chain obligations.

1 — Reuse

Redeploy internally or donate via verified programmes. Data-wiped and certified. Highest-value disposition — avoids both manufacturing a new device and processing the old one as waste.

2 — Refurbish

Repair, upgrade, and resell through certified refurbishment channels. Extends useful life, captures residual value, avoids landfill. Second-best to reuse — but significantly better than recycling.

3 — Recycle

Certified responsible recycling with data destruction evidence. Last resort only — recovers materials but cannot recover the embodied energy and carbon already committed to the device's manufacture.

The ITAD governance question

Can your organisation produce a data-verified certificate of destruction or reuse for every disposed device? If not, you have both a data security gap and a regulatory exposure — not only an environmental one. ITAD is where asset lifecycle governance, data protection, and sustainability converge.

Section 5

Allocation pitfalls — two things that go wrong silently.

Once you have evidence in hand, two problems regularly erode its quality without being obvious from the number itself. Both are methodology questions that must be asked explicitly — the answer affects not just accuracy but what operational levers are visible.

Pitfall 1 — Double counting

The same emissions appearing in multiple places in your inventory.

Your numbers look more complete while becoming less accurate — and the error is hard to spot without a systematic boundary review.

Common examples
  • →Counting full data centre facility emissions AND cloud provider emissions for workloads in the same facility
  • →Counting embodied device emissions AND the same upstream manufacturing emissions in a broader procurement category
RemedyDocument source boundaries explicitly. Count each emission source once, in the most specific and accurate location available.
Pitfall 2 — Misleading allocation denominators

Suppliers allocate shared emissions using different bases — each gives a materially different result.

Server-hours, VM-hours, vCPU-hours, energy consumption, revenue, or a proprietary method. An allocation detached from energy use — revenue-based, for example — does not reward efficient consumption and can obscure operational levers.

The question to always ask

What is the allocation basis, and does it reflect physical consumption? An allocation based on contracted capacity rather than metered IT load penalises efficient tenants and rewards wasteful ones.

GoalNot perfection — avoiding false precision. Document the methodology, note its limitations, and build a roadmap for improvement.
How allocation basis changes your number — same infrastructure, same total emissions, three different answers

Scenario: A shared data centre hosts 4 tenants. Total annual emissions: 1,000 tCO₂e. Your organisation is Tenant C.

Tenant Revenue-based Server-hours Metered IT load
Tenant A 400 t 150 t 100 t
Tenant B 300 t 300 t 350 t
Tenant C ← you 200 t 400 t 450 t
Tenant D 100 t 150 t 100 t
2.25× range — Tenant C's allocated emissions range from 200t to 450t depending solely on methodology choice. Revenue-based allocation rewards commercial activity, not operational efficiency. Metered IT load rewards efficient tenants. Always ask which basis was used and whether it reflects physical consumption.

Knowledge Check · Module 7 · Q1

A hardware vendor's proposal includes a product carbon footprint figure supported by a "third-party reviewed" verification statement. Before accepting this as high-confidence Scope 3 data, what is the most important clarification to seek?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

"Reviewed" and "independently assured" are not equivalent — and the difference matters significantly for how you use the data. A reviewed figure means an independent party checked that the methodology was correctly applied — but they did not audit the underlying data. An assured figure means the data, methodology, and calculations were all independently verified against a stated standard.

Before recording this as high-confidence Scope 3 data, you need to know: what did the reviewing party actually examine, what standard were they working to, and does their scope cover the specific model and configuration you are procuring?

Knowledge Check · Module 7 · Q2

A procurement team receives a response from a cloud provider stating: "We are committed to running on 100% renewable energy and have net-zero targets validated by SBTi." What should the procurement team request in addition to this?

Explanation — correct answer: B

The provider's statement describes their corporate sustainability trajectory — their ambition and direction. It says nothing about the specific service you are buying. For Scope 3 reporting, you need product-level evidence: what is the carbon intensity of the specific region where your workloads run, how are shared infrastructure emissions allocated to your tenancy, and what instruments support the renewable energy claim?

Option A is the same category of information as what was already provided — more corporate reporting, not product-level data. Options C and D are legitimate governance signals but do not address the Scope 3 measurement requirement. The gap the procurement team is carrying is product-level evidence, not corporate commitment evidence. Both matter. Neither substitutes for the other.

Module 7 — Key Takeaways

◆
Corporate commitments and product evidence are different things.

A net-zero commitment tells you about the supplier's trajectory. A product carbon footprint tells you about this transaction. Both matter; neither substitutes for the other.

◆
When you receive a lifecycle assessment, ask seven questions.

Methodology, lifecycle stages, emission factors, use-phase assumptions, upstream data sources, functional unit, and assurance status. Without these, a number is not evidence.

◆
Self-declared, reviewed, and independently assured are not equivalent.

Treating them as the same is a significant data quality error. Your vendor evidence register should record which tier applies to each figure you hold.

◆
Every purchasing decision is a signal.

When you buy without asking for evidence, you signal that evidence is not required. Over time, those signals shape what the market supplies.

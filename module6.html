Software Engineering
and Application Sustainability

Infrastructure decisions affect how efficiently you run a workload. Software decisions determine whether the workload needs to exist at all — and what it costs to execute when it does.

Section 1

Enterprise architecture as a sustainability lever.

Before code, before infrastructure — the application portfolio. Every application that continues running because nobody made a deliberate decision to retire it is consuming resources for no business return. Portfolio rationalisation is the highest-leverage action in most estates.

The TIME model applied to sustainability — click each category to explore the questions and actions

Select a category to see the sustainability questions to ask and the actions it requires.

The carbon you do not generate by not running an application outweighs any optimisation you could apply to it. Eliminate is the highest-leverage category — and the most politically difficult. Every blocked retirement is a governance conversation waiting to happen.

The portfolio is the programme

Without active portfolio management, applications accumulate. They are added when a need arises and rarely retired when that need diminishes. The result is a portfolio that grows by default, consuming compute, licence cost, and operational attention for no corresponding value. This is not primarily a technical problem — it is a governance one.

Section 2

Sustainable software development — the SCI metric.

Software behaviour is one of the most underestimated sustainability levers in enterprise IT. Efficiency improvements at the code level compound across every execution. The Software Carbon Intensity (SCI) specification gives engineering teams a metric that maps directly to their decisions.

SCI is specified by the Green Software Foundation. The functional unit must match what the software actually produces: a transaction, a user session, an API call, a gigabyte processed. Changing the functional unit changes the number — define it before comparing.

Where in the development lifecycle does sustainability fit?

Design (architecture and data model choices), development (code efficiency and test coverage of sustainability acceptance criteria), test (SCI measurement as a gate, not an afterthought), deploy (infrastructure right-sizing at provisioning), and operate (ongoing SCI monitoring). Sustainability acceptance criteria belong in the definition of done — not in the quarterly sustainability review.

Section 3

Data management as a sustainability practice.

Data growth is the silent driver behind storage costs, replication overhead, and security exposure. Treating storage growth as a performance metric with an owner — not a technical inevitability — is what separates organisations that manage data from those that accumulate it.

Default behaviours that accumulate
  • ⚠Logs retained at maximum resolution indefinitely
  • ⚠Multiple uncompressed copies across environments
  • ⚠Data replicated to regions "just in case"
  • ⚠Dark data — stored, protected, and never accessed
  • ⚠No lifecycle policy — the default is to keep everything
Governance actions that change the default
  • ✓Treat storage growth as a KPI with a named owner
  • ✓Define retention tiers: hot / warm / cold / delete
  • ✓Log resolution and sampling matched to operational need
  • ✓Data minimisation as a design constraint — collect only what you will use
  • ✓Active deletion policy: data that has passed its retention date is deleted, not archived indefinitely

Section 4

The SaaS measurement challenge.

Multi-tenant SaaS platforms share infrastructure across many customers. The allocation methodology used by the provider materially affects what you see — and the absence of data does not mean the absence of impact. Asking the right questions at contract renewal is how Scope 3 coverage gets built.

Questions to ask any SaaS vendor — click each to see why it matters and what a good answer looks like

The absence of data is not absence of impact — it is absence of measurement. A vendor who cannot answer these questions is exposing you to unquantified Scope 3 emissions in your inventory. Use contract renewal as the lever to require improvement over time.

The portfolio question — before any SaaS procurement decision

Think about the application portfolio you work with. If you had to classify every application using the TIME model today — Tolerate, Invest, Migrate, Eliminate — what proportion would honestly be in the Eliminate category? The answer in most organisations is 20–40%. What is stopping those decisions being made is almost never technical. It is political.

Knowledge Check · Module 6 · Q1

A product team wants to track the sustainability of their service. Their application serves 2 million transactions per day and is growing at 40% annually. Which metric should they prioritise for engineering accountability?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

A service growing at 40% annually will almost certainly emit more in total each year — even if the team is doing excellent sustainability work. If the metric is total emissions, the engineering team can never show improvement against business growth. Their incentive becomes constraining growth, which is not the right goal.

Software Carbon Intensity — carbon per transaction — decouples sustainability performance from business scale. If the service processes 40% more transactions but emits the same or less carbon per transaction, the engineering team has genuinely improved. That is the accountability signal worth tracking.

Knowledge Check · Module 6 · Q2

A SaaS vendor states in their annual sustainability report that they are "committed to net zero by 2040 across all operations." What additional information should you request before including this in your Scope 3 reporting?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

A corporate net-zero commitment tells you about the supplier's trajectory and ambition. It tells you almost nothing about the specific emissions associated with your tenancy of their service. Scope 3 reporting requires product-level evidence — not corporate commitments.

The questions you need answered are: where is the service hosted, how is the infrastructure powered, how are shared infrastructure emissions allocated to individual tenants, and how confident are you in those allocation figures? The absence of that data does not mean the absence of impact — it means you have an unquantified Scope 3 exposure.

Module 6 — Key Takeaways

◆
Enterprise architecture is a sustainability governance mechanism.

Every application that runs without a deliberate decision to keep it running is consuming resources for no return. Retiring applications is often the single highest-leverage action in a portfolio review.

◆
Software Carbon Intensity is the metric engineering teams can directly influence.

Intensity — carbon per transaction, per call, per session — moves with engineering decisions. Total emissions move with business volume. Both matter; they serve different purposes.

◆
Energy efficiency and software quality point in the same direction.

Efficient algorithms, event-driven patterns, streaming over bulk loading, filter-at-source queries — these are engineering quality practices that also reduce energy per unit of useful work.

◆
Dark data is one of the most easily remediated waste categories.

It accumulates through permissive defaults and the absence of any process asking whether retained data is still serving a purpose. Data minimisation at design stage is the intervention point.

◆
SaaS absence of data is not absence of impact.

Multi-tenant platforms carry Scope 3 exposure whether or not the provider discloses it. Ask the right questions at contract evaluation and renewal — not retrospectively during a reporting cycle.

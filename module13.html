Sustainability by IT:
Enabling Positive Impact Beyond the Estate

Section 1

Footprint and handprint — both dimensions matter.

Everything in Modules 1–12 was about reducing the negative impact of the IT estate — the footprint. This module is the other side of the equation: how technology creates positive sustainability outcomes beyond its own boundary. The handprint. And why it only lands credibly when your footprint is already under discipline.

Footprint — the impact dimension

The environmental impact of running the IT estate. Energy, water, materials, and emissions from data centres, cloud, end-user devices, software, and supply chains.

Status: Maturing. Measurement frameworks, regulatory requirements, and operational practices are established. Most organisations are somewhere on this journey.
Handprint — the enablement dimension

The positive sustainability outcomes that technology enables beyond its own boundary. Avoided emissions. Resource efficiency in other sectors. Social outcomes enabled by digital access and transparency.

Status: Emerging. Methodologies are less mature. Attribution is harder. But the scale of the opportunity exceeds the footprint dimension for most organisations.

The key is connecting applications to measurable outcomes — not describing them aspirationally. Each item is only as credible as its measurement. Scope 4 requires honest methodology and documented assumptions.

Section 2

Scope 4 — avoided emissions.

Scope 4 is the formal mechanism for measuring avoided emissions — the positive sustainability value that technology creates outside the IT estate. Imperfect measurement that is honest is more credible than no measurement.

Builds on Module 3 ↗

Scope 4 sits alongside the Scope 1/2/3 framework introduced in Module 3. It is supplementary guidance from the GHG Protocol, not a standalone standard — but it uses the same measurement rigour.

How Scope 4 avoided emissions are calculated

1
Define the counterfactual

What behaviour is the technology replacing? The counterfactual must be realistic and specific — not the worst-case alternative. A route optimisation platform: the counterfactual is the same deliveries without route optimisation, using average fleet emission factors.

2
Quantify the avoided activity

How much of the replaced behaviour occurs at what emission factor? Kilometres not driven, flights not taken, energy not consumed in a building, agricultural inputs not applied. Each requires specific data, not estimates from industry averages where better data exists.

3
Calculate avoided emissions

Avoided activity × emission factor of the replaced behaviour = gross avoided emissions. Then subtract the emissions generated by the technology platform itself — the net avoided emissions.

4
Document and disclose assumptions transparently

State the counterfactual. State the data sources. State the confidence level. Imperfect measurement with documented assumptions is defensible. Undocumented estimates are not.

Section 3

Innovation, CleanTech, and the commercial opportunity.

Technology organisations that have built genuine operational capability in sustainable IT are better positioned to develop commercial sustainability solutions. The insight from actually managing energy consumption, hardware lifecycle, carbon accounting, and supply chain evidence at scale is the same insight needed to build platforms that help other organisations do the same.

Innovation, CleanTech, and the commercial opportunity — three pathways
Internal innovation

Structured hackathons focused on specific operational problems — with clear criteria and implementation support for winning ideas — surface solutions the central programme would never identify.

The insight from managing energy and Scope 3 at scale translates directly into platforms that other organisations need.

CleanTech partnerships

Technology organisations that have built real GreenOps capability become credible partners for CleanTech ventures. The operational experience is the differentiator — not the aspiration.

Carbon accounting platforms, energy optimisation tools, circular economy tracking — all need technology partners with operational credibility.

Customer differentiation

Enterprise procurement increasingly includes sustainability criteria. Organisations with measurable, evidenced sustainability performance win contracts that others cannot. This is happening now — not in five years.

The same work that reduces cost and improves governance also satisfies supplier questionnaires and ESG screening requirements.

Knowledge check — question 1 of 2

An organisation wants to report Scope 4 avoided emissions from its route optimisation platform. The platform reduced average vehicle distance by 11%. What is the correct next step after quantifying the avoided activity?

Explanation

The five-step Scope 4 method requires: establish baseline behaviour, define the technology intervention, quantify avoided activity, apply an emissions factor, then state a confidence level and assumptions. The confidence factor is essential because not all of the reduction may be attributable to the platform — fuel price changes, regulatory pressure, or driver behaviour changes may have contributed. In the course worked example, a 15% confidence adjustment is applied, reducing 1,490 tCO₂e gross to 1,267 tCO₂e reported.

Option A overstates the claim by omitting the attribution adjustment — this is a greenwashing risk. Option B is incorrect: Scope 4 is a recognised category in the GHG Protocol supplementary guidance, increasingly used for technology-enabled avoided emissions claims. Option D misclassifies the emission — avoided emissions from customer or partner operations are not Scope 1, which covers the organisation's own direct emissions.

Knowledge check — question 2 of 2

A technology director says: "Our handprint is larger than our footprint — our sustainability story should lead with the positive impact we enable." What is the most important challenge to this position?

Explanation

The sequence matters. Footprint discipline is the non-negotiable foundation — organisations that lead with their handprint before demonstrating genuine footprint reduction are exposed to the accusation of using positive impact claims to distract from unmanaged negative impact. That is greenwashing by narrative, even when the handprint claims are legitimate.

Option A overstates the restriction — Scope 4 and handprint claims are accepted when documented with methodology. Option C is too pessimistic: attribution uncertainty is manageable through confidence levels and stated assumptions. Option D is partially right about the board audience, but misses the sequencing requirement — the handprint is a compelling story once the footprint is credibly under management, not before it.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Take 5–10 minutes. Specificity matters more than completeness.

1Think about the products or services your organisation provides using technology. Is there a credible Scope 4 claim — avoided emissions enabled by your technology? If yes, is it currently measured? If not, what would a first pass calculation require?
2Does your organisation currently distinguish between its footprint narrative and its handprint narrative in external communications? Is the sequence correct — footprint credibility established before handprint claims?
3Where is technology already creating a measurable positive sustainability outcome in your organisation's operations or products — even if it's not currently framed or measured as such?

Key Takeaways

◆
The handprint dimension is often the story that resonates most at board level.

Technology as an enabler of broader sustainability goals — not just a problem to be managed — is increasingly a component of how organisations report and are evaluated.

◆
Applications must be connected to measurable outcomes.

Carbon reporting platforms, smart buildings, route optimisation, Product-as-a-Service, supply chain traceability — each is only as credible as its measurement.

◆
Scope 4 is the formal mechanism for measuring avoided emissions.

Quantify the counterfactual. Estimate emissions avoided with stated assumptions. Be transparent about confidence. Imperfect measurement that is honest is more credible than no measurement.

◆
The handprint argument requires footprint credibility first.

Sequence matters. A handprint claim from an organisation that cannot speak to its own footprint is not credible — and will not survive scrutiny.

Section 1

Domain fluency — why one narrative doesn't work.

The most common mistake is applying the same sustainability narrative everywhere. Each domain has different dominant drivers, different failure modes, and different levers. GreenOps requires domain fluency — not a universal story.

End-User Services: the footprint is mostly before you switch it on

~80% of a laptop's lifetime footprint is manufacturing — before it reaches your desk. The dominant lever in end-user services is lifecycle and procurement, not plug socket behaviour. Every additional year you keep a device in productive use represents a meaningful reduction in embodied carbon per year of service.

⚠ Common defaults that add up
  • Extra devices issued as standard regardless of role
  • Two monitors by default — whether the role needs them or not
  • Docking stations accumulating in storage rooms, never recovered
  • Laptops shipped by air because onboarding was left to the last minute
  • No recovery process — ordering new is easier than redeploying existing
✓ What good practice looks like
  • Role-based device standards — match specification to actual role requirements
  • Power management enforced centrally by policy, not individual choice
  • Recovery and redeploy process genuinely easier than ordering new
  • Accessory lifecycle management — cables and docks are assets, not consumables
  • Governed AI usage for routine tasks to prevent silent inference growth

The primary lever

Lifecycle extension. One additional year of use per device reduces embodied carbon per year of service by ~20% on a typical refresh cycle.

Data Centres: where resource intensity is concentrated and measurable

Data centres are where the numbers are easy to quote — and easy to misuse. PUE without utilisation data is dangerous. A lightly loaded site can have good PUE while still being wasteful in absolute terms.

⚠ Metric theatre to watch for
  • Design PUE presented as if it reflects operational reality
  • Annual average PUE hiding seasonal peaks (ask for seasonal breakdown)
  • Portfolio averages masking the worst-performing sites
  • "Renewable energy" claims without stated instruments or boundaries
✓ The metrics — what they measure and what they hide
  • PUE — Total facility power ÷ IT equipment power. ~1.1 exceptional. Context matters.
  • WUE — Litres of water ÷ kWh of IT energy. Track seasonality and water stress.
  • CUE — CO₂ emissions ÷ IT equipment energy. Bridge between efficiency and impact.
  • REF/ERF — Only as good as the evidence behind them. Push for granularity.

The follow-up question

For any PUE figure: what is IT server utilisation — and what is the plan to improve it through consolidation and decommissioning?

Cloud: the continuous control loop

Cloud doesn't remove infrastructure responsibility — it changes where the levers sit. Rightsizing is not a project. It's a discipline that only sticks when owned jointly by platform engineering and FinOps, with application owners brought in on decisions that affect performance.

⚠ Where cloud waste actually hides
  • Zombie resources — running with no active workload
  • Non-production environments left on after business hours
  • Orphaned storage from decommissioned services
  • Oversized instances based on peak provisioning, never revisited
  • Untagged resources with no owner and no review trigger
✓ The 5-step remediation loop
  • Visibility and attribution — if you can't attribute, you can't govern
  • Baselining unit economics — carbon per VM-hour, idle percentage
  • Remediate with a hierarchy of levers — kill zombies first, then rightsize
  • Prevent recurrence with guardrails — policy-as-code, default sizing limits
  • Verify and institutionalise — bake into FinOps reviews, not heroic side missions

Software and data: decisions that compound over a service's lifetime

Software behaviour is one of the most underestimated sustainability levers. Inefficient code patterns, unchecked data growth, and ungoverned telemetry aggregate into material footprints at scale — and are rarely attributed to the engineering decisions that drove them.

⚠ Common patterns to watch for
  • Unnecessary computation on every request — cache-first thinking is absent
  • Data retained indefinitely — no lifecycle policy, no review trigger
  • Heavy dependencies for lightweight tasks
  • Telemetry collecting everything "just in case" with no retention policy
  • Applications that continue running because nobody decided to retire them
✓ What shifts the software footprint
  • Compute efficiency — measure carbon per transaction, not just total compute
  • Software Carbon Intensity (SCI) — intensity, not absolute, is the actionable metric
  • Architecture-level choices — right pattern for the workload, not the familiar one
  • Data minimisation — only collect, process, and retain what's justified
  • Portfolio rationalisation — the TIME model applied to carbon

Hardware lifecycle: starts at design, not disposal

Embodied carbon is determined by procurement and use decisions made years before end-of-life. Evidence-based refresh cycles and circular economy criteria are the lever. The recycling bin is not.

⚠ Three lifecycle trade-off traps
  • Trap 1 — Extending asset life isn't always right: if older hardware is dramatically less energy-efficient, keeping it longer may increase operational energy per unit of useful compute
  • Trap 2 — Refresh early "in the name of efficiency": if you replace too frequently, embodied emissions and e-waste rise
  • Trap 3 — Comparing products without comparable functional units: a higher-footprint server may be more efficient per unit of delivered compute
✓ The lifecycle governance approach
  • Treat lifecycle decisions as a unit economics problem: energy per unit of useful work, balanced against embodied impact and lifespan
  • Include sustainability criteria in procurement — not just performance and cost
  • Design for repairability and component replacement, not whole-unit replacement
  • ITAD governance — certified disposal, data sanitisation, refurbishment pathways
  • Manufacturer take-back programmes for responsible end-of-life

AI: the cross-cutting consumption multiplier

The main risk for most organisations is not the training runs that make headlines. It's inference demand scaling quietly across hundreds or thousands of users — embedded in products, integrated into workflows, and left ungoverned because it feels like productivity rather than infrastructure.

AI is the fastest-growing ungoverned consumption category in enterprise IT.

The governance failure pattern

A large frontier model deployed as the default for all staff — including for writing short emails. Routine tasks using models 10–50× over-specified. Inference demand scales at the rate of adoption, not value.

The fix: model tiering

Small models for routine tasks, mid-range where reasoning is needed, frontier only where it genuinely changes the outcome. Make the smallest reliable model the default. Require justification to escalate.

Networks: the invisible footprint

Networks are the invisible infrastructure of digital services. Their impact is frequently omitted from sustainability programmes because the data is hard to access. That doesn't make the impact smaller.

Egress costs and egress footprint move together. If cloud egress charges are significant, data is moving in patterns that were not intentionally designed. Cost and carbon align without additional justification.

✓ Network efficiency levers
  • Minimise unnecessary data movement — every API call transferring excess data aggregates at scale
  • Reduce cross-region replication to what's justified — revisit defaults set under different constraints
  • Cache at the appropriate layer — repeated transfers of static content are avoidable
  • Review video conferencing defaults — high-resolution by default is a policy choice, not a technical requirement
  • Treat egress costs and footprint as the same signal — they are

Section 3

AI consumption — what to stop and start doing.

These are the defaults that silently burn compute for no proportionate value, and the disciplines that let AI scale without becoming a silent waste engine. Neither list is a restriction on using AI well.

What to stop doing
  • Using the biggest model by default when a smaller one is fit for purpose
  • Stuffing the context window with entire documents that aren't needed in full
  • Letting chat threads grow indefinitely — long threads carry silent context overhead
  • Asking vague questions and iterating five times — be specific from the start
  • Generating long outputs you won't use — enforce a length limit
  • Using AI where a search query, template, or rules-based workflow would do the job
  • Running non-urgent AI jobs in peak-carbon periods out of habit
  • Treating AI consumption as ungoverned — if you can't see usage, you can't manage it
What to start doing
  • Right-size models by use case — smallest reliable model as the default
  • Measure AI consumption: calls per user, tokens per request, cost per call
  • Use templates and default to short outputs — design for efficient behaviour
  • Batch and cache wherever it makes sense — repeated queries shouldn't regenerate
  • Treat non-urgent AI as flexible load — schedule for lower-carbon windows
  • Make product owners accountable for AI unit economics — value per inference
  • Require governance sign-off before deploying frontier models as organisational defaults

Knowledge Check · Module 4 · Q1

An IT manager presents data centre sustainability performance using the portfolio average PUE of 1.45 and states the estate is running on "renewable energy." What two follow-up questions should you ask?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option A

Portfolio average PUE is one of the most commonly misused metrics in IT sustainability. It blends best and worst sites, hides seasonal variation, and — crucially — tells you nothing about whether the IT equipment being powered is actually doing useful work. A lightly loaded estate can show a respectable PUE while consuming significant energy for very little computational output. The first follow-up is always: what is server utilisation, and what's the plan to improve it?

The renewable energy question matters equally. "Renewable energy" can mean very different things: location-based grid renewables, purchased certificates with varying quality, power purchase agreements for dedicated capacity, or on-site generation. Without knowing which instruments are in use and what their boundary is, the claim is narrative rather than evidence.

Knowledge Check · Module 4 · Q2

A product team is deploying a large frontier AI model as the default assistant for all staff, including for writing short emails and basic search tasks. What is the most accurate GreenOps assessment of this decision?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

The inference demand from a large frontier model is materially higher than from a smaller, fine-tuned model performing an equivalent task. When that model is deployed as the default for everything — including tasks that could be handled by something far lighter — inference consumption scales at the rate of adoption, not at the rate of genuine value delivered.

This is a governance failure, not a technical one. The fix is model tiering: a framework that assigns model types to task categories, makes the smallest fit-for-purpose model the default, and requires a clear justification to escalate to a heavier model.

Module 4 — Key Takeaways

◆
Different domains need different lenses.

The biggest lever in end-user services is lifecycle. In cloud, it's utilisation discipline. In software, it's compute per transaction. Applying one narrative everywhere produces poor decisions.

◆
PUE without utilisation is dangerous.

A lightly loaded data centre can show a respectable PUE and still be wasteful in absolute terms. Always ask about server utilisation alongside facility efficiency metrics.

◆
Software decisions compound over a service's lifetime.

Inefficient code patterns, unchecked data growth, and ungoverned telemetry aggregate into material footprints at scale — and are rarely attributed to the engineering decisions that drove them.

◆
Hardware lifecycle governance starts at design, not disposal.

Embodied carbon is determined by procurement and use decisions made years before end-of-life. Evidence-based refresh cycles and circular economy criteria are the lever — not the recycling bin.

◆
AI inference is the fastest-growing ungoverned consumption category.

The risk is not headline training jobs. It's routine inference at scale, embedded in workflows, consuming compute for no proportionate value because nobody has made governance the default.

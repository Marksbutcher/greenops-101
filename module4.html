Utilisation is not the same as efficiency

A server running at 90% utilisation is not necessarily efficient. If it is running the wrong workload, in the wrong region, on hardware that should have been refreshed three years ago, it is waste at high utilisation. Efficiency requires the right workload, in the right place, on appropriately specified hardware — not just high utilisation numbers.

PUE = Total facility power ÷ IT equipment power. Lower is better (1.0 = perfect). WUE = litres of water per kWh of IT load. CUE = CO₂ ÷ IT equipment energy. All illustrative scenarios — real values vary by facility design, climate, and workload.

The metric theatre problem

Most organisations don't fail because they lack metrics. They fail because they use metrics as theatre. Design PUE presented as operational reality. Annual averages hiding summer peaks. Portfolio averages protecting the worst sites. Renewable claims without stated instruments. If the metric can't be traced to an operational lever — treat it as storytelling.

Section 3

Where cloud waste actually hides.

Organisations routinely over-focus on rightsizing individual instances while leaving larger, easier wins untouched. Work the hierarchy from top to bottom — quick wins first, structural levers second.

Cloud waste hierarchy — ranked by ease of elimination vs carbon impact. Click any row.

Rightsizing is not a project — it is a discipline. It only sticks when owned jointly by platform engineering and FinOps, with guardrails (policy-as-code for tagging, default size limits, mandatory TTL for non-production) preventing recurrence.

Cloud does not eliminate the footprint — it moves it to Scope 3

Moving workloads to cloud moves IT emissions from Scope 1 and 2 (direct operations) into Scope 3 (supply chain). The emissions still exist. They are now your cloud provider's operational emissions, allocated to your consumption. A shared responsibility model applies: providers improve infrastructure efficiency; customers govern what they run and where.

Section 4

Hardware lifecycle — the break-even decision.

Embodied carbon is front-loaded at manufacture and largely fixed once a device exists. Use-phase carbon accumulates gradually. The decision to extend or replace depends on which side of the break-even point you are on — and that calculation changes with grid intensity and workload.

Lifecycle carbon arc — embodied vs operational. Drag the slider to move the replacement point.
Replace at year: Year 3

Illustrative model — actual break-even depends on device category, workload, grid intensity, and embodied carbon of replacement hardware. The principle holds: extending use reduces embodied carbon per year; the question is whether operational energy cost outweighs that saving at your grid intensity.

Trap 1
"Extend always"

Usually right — but if older hardware is dramatically less efficient for the workload, keeping it longer can increase operational energy per unit of output. Compare on a functional basis, not a headline figure.

Trap 2
"Refresh for efficiency"

Replacing too frequently raises embodied emissions and e-waste. Improved operational metrics can mask rising lifecycle impact. Embodied carbon doesn't appear in operational dashboards — but it is real and material.

Trap 3
"Compare on specs"

A higher-footprint server may still be more efficient per unit of delivered compute if it replaces multiple older machines. Compare on functional unit — compute per watt per unit of useful work — not headline product footprint.

End-user services are often the largest category nobody manages

End-user devices represent 20-30% of most enterprise IT footprints. Device procurement decisions — which model, what refresh cycle, whether to refurbish — have a larger impact than most organisations realise. Yet these decisions are rarely governed with sustainability criteria. The embodied carbon is committed at procurement, not at end of life.

Knowledge Check · Module 4 · Q1

An IT manager presents data centre sustainability performance using the portfolio average PUE of 1.45 and states the estate is running on "renewable energy." What two follow-up questions should you ask?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option A

Portfolio average PUE is one of the most commonly misused metrics in IT sustainability. It blends best and worst sites, hides seasonal variation, and — crucially — tells you nothing about whether the IT equipment being powered is actually doing useful work. A lightly loaded estate can show a respectable PUE while consuming significant energy for very little computational output. The first follow-up is always: what is server utilisation, and what's the plan to improve it?

The renewable energy question matters equally. "Renewable energy" can mean very different things: location-based grid renewables, purchased certificates with varying quality, power purchase agreements for dedicated capacity, or on-site generation. Without knowing which instruments are in use and what their boundary is, the claim is narrative rather than evidence.

Knowledge Check · Module 4 · Q2

A product team is deploying a large frontier AI model as the default assistant for all staff, including for writing short emails and basic search tasks. What is the most accurate GreenOps assessment of this decision?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

The inference demand from a large frontier model is materially higher than from a smaller, fine-tuned model performing an equivalent task. When that model is deployed as the default for everything — including tasks that could be handled by something far lighter — inference consumption scales at the rate of adoption, not at the rate of genuine value delivered.

This is a governance failure, not a technical one. The fix is model tiering: a framework that assigns model types to task categories, makes the smallest fit-for-purpose model the default, and requires a clear justification to escalate to a heavier model.

Module 4 — Key Takeaways

◆
Different domains need different lenses.

The biggest lever in end-user services is lifecycle. In cloud, it's utilisation discipline. In software, it's compute per transaction. Applying one narrative everywhere produces poor decisions.

◆
PUE without utilisation is dangerous.

A lightly loaded data centre can show a respectable PUE and still be wasteful in absolute terms. Always ask about server utilisation alongside facility efficiency metrics.

◆
Software decisions compound over a service's lifetime.

Inefficient code patterns, unchecked data growth, and ungoverned telemetry aggregate into material footprints at scale — and are rarely attributed to the engineering decisions that drove them.

◆
Hardware lifecycle governance starts at design, not disposal.

Embodied carbon is determined by procurement and use decisions made years before end-of-life. Evidence-based refresh cycles and circular economy criteria are the lever — not the recycling bin.

◆
AI inference is the fastest-growing ungoverned consumption category.

The risk is not headline training jobs. It's routine inference at scale, embedded in workflows, consuming compute for no proportionate value because nobody has made governance the default.

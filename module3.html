Section 1

Where programmes build credibility — or lose it.

Measurement is the dividing line. This module gives you the minimum fluency needed — not to become a carbon accountant, but to understand what the numbers mean, where they come from, and how to spot the ones that mislead.

The measurement credibility test

Can you explain what each number in your sustainability report is measuring, how it was calculated, what the confidence level is, and what operational decision it informs? If the answer to any of those is "I'm not sure," you have a measurement credibility gap.

Section 2

Activity-based vs spend-based measurement.

These are not equally good methods with different trade-offs. Spend-based is acceptable as a starting point. It is genuinely weak as an operational tool.

Activity-Based Measurement
Uses real physical drivers — energy in kWh, number of devices, server hours, VM-hours, storage capacity, data transferred. Linked to emission factors by location and time. Closer to operational truth.
Strengths
  • Reflects actual consumption
  • Supports operational optimisation
  • Can distinguish utilisation and location differences
  • Enables meaningful trend analysis
Requirements
  • Requires actual data feeds from systems
  • Consistent definitions across the estate
  • Higher data collection burden initially
Spend-Based Measurement
Financial expenditure multiplied by average emission factors. Scales well because procurement systems have spend data even where operational systems don't. Acceptable for Scope 3 baseline reporting — weak as an optimisation tool.
Where it works
  • Scope 3 first-pass baseline (no better data available)
  • Supplier categories without activity data
  • Initial portfolio-level sizing
What it hides
  • Utilisation differences between teams
  • Geography and carbon intensity differences
  • Time-of-day and grid condition variations
  • Whether the workload was necessary at all

Why it matters in practice

If two teams both spend £1m on cloud, spend-based methods report similar emissions. In reality, one could be running efficient workloads at low carbon intensity in Scotland; the other wasting resources on idle infrastructure in a coal-heavy region. The same spend. Dramatically different actual footprint. Only activity-based measurement reveals this — and therefore only activity-based measurement can drive the right operational decision.

Carbon intensity is not constant.

The same workload in two different places — or at two different times of day — can have a dramatically different physical footprint. Region selection is one of the most underestimated levers in enterprise GreenOps.

Location-based reporting

Physical grid reality

Reflects the average carbon intensity of the grid where consumption actually occurs. Tells you your real physical grid impact — the operationally honest number. If you run in a coal-heavy region, intensity is high.

Market-based reporting

Contractual instruments

Reflects renewable energy certificates or PPAs. Matters for corporate reporting commitments. Critical caveat: buying a certificate doesn't change which electrons power your workload. GreenOps requires physical reality as its reference point.

Carbon also varies by time of day

A grid that draws heavily on solar at midday but switches to gas peaking plants in the evening shows substantially different intensity hour by hour. Running a batch workload at 2am on a renewable-heavy grid is meaningfully different from running it at 7pm peak. This is the foundation of temporal shifting, covered in Module 5.

Section 4

Unit metrics — the operational tool.

Total emissions figures matter for compliance — they're the rear-view mirror. Unit metrics are what tell you whether the intensity of what you're doing is improving, even as the business grows. The right metric depends on your role.

Product & App Owners
Carbon per transaction Carbon per user session Carbon per API call

Maps to engineering decisions and service behaviour. The metric a product owner can directly influence through architecture, code efficiency, and data management choices.

Platform & Cloud Teams
Carbon per VM-hour Carbon per container-hour Idle percentage Region carbon mix

Maps to provisioning and utilisation decisions. Platform teams control rightsizing, scheduling, and region selection — the primary levers for cloud carbon intensity.

Data Centre Teams
PUE WUE CUE REF / ERF Server utilisation

Maps to facility efficiency and operational risk. Critical note: PUE without utilisation data is dangerous. Always ask about server utilisation alongside facility efficiency metrics.

Technology Leadership
Carbon intensity of top services % estate on activity-based measurement Supplier evidence coverage

Maps to portfolio decisions and governance. The metrics that connect operational reality to strategic choices, investment decisions, and disclosure obligations.

Section 5

Establishing a baseline.

Every credible sustainability programme starts here. Without a baseline, you have no way to know whether anything is improving — and no basis for setting targets that can actually be verified.

01
IT Asset Registry

A structured inventory of what you own, run, and consume as a service. On-premises data centres, owned and leased end-user devices, network equipment, cloud and SaaS services, outsourced IT operations. Captures quantity, location, and actual consumption data where available.

02
Emissions Table

For each asset category, identify emission sources and apply appropriate emission factors for that geography and time period. Document the boundary clearly — what's in scope, what's excluded and why. Sum to a total with documented confidence levels.

03
Hybrid Approach for First Pass

Most first baselines mix activity data (metered energy, device counts, cloud billing) with spend-based estimates where supplier data is missing. That's fine. Document confidence levels and build a clear plan for migrating from spend proxies to activity data as the programme matures.

04
Target State Simulation

Once you have a baseline year, simulate your target state. What does the footprint look like if you achieve your planned optimisations — rightsizing, extended device lifespans, reduced dark data? This becomes a multi-year model linking current state to commitments.

A warning about baselines

A baseline that's directional and honest is better than one that's precise and shaky. False precision in headline figures — achieved by using spend-based proxies everywhere and stating them as accurate — creates a credibility problem when someone with technical knowledge examines the methodology. Document confidence levels. Acknowledge gaps. Build a migration plan.

Knowledge Check · Module 3 · Q1

Two engineering teams each spend £1 million per year on cloud infrastructure. A spend-based emissions model reports similar footprints for both. Why might this be seriously misleading?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

Spend-based measurement treats equal expenditure as equal footprint. But £1m spent efficiently in a low-carbon region with high utilisation is not the same as £1m spent on idle infrastructure in a high-carbon region. Spend doesn't tell you utilisation. It doesn't tell you location. It doesn't tell you time-of-day. And it doesn't tell you whether the workload was necessary at all.

This is why spend-based measurement is acceptable as a baseline starting point for Scope 3 reporting — where you have no other data — but is genuinely weak as an operational tool. GreenOps requires the physical reality, because that's the only thing you can actually improve.

Knowledge Check · Module 3 · Q2

A cloud architect is choosing between two equivalent regions for a new workload. Region A has a carbon intensity of 20g CO₂/kWh. Region B has 580g CO₂/kWh. Everything else being equal, what is the correct GreenOps framing of this decision?

✓ Correct: Option B

A 29× difference in carbon intensity is not an incremental improvement — it's a transformation. Running an equivalent workload in a low-carbon region versus a high-carbon region can reduce the operational carbon footprint by an order of magnitude before you've changed a single line of code. This is real, measurable, and sits within engineering's remit.

Module 3 — Key Takeaways

◆
Scope 3 is usually the biggest and least-measured category.

Cloud, hardware, managed services, and end-of-life typically account for 70–80% of IT footprint. Most organisations have very limited visibility into it.

◆
Spend-based measurement is a starting point, not an operational tool.

It hides utilisation, location, time-of-day, and whether workloads were necessary. GreenOps needs physical reality, not financial proxies.

◆
Where and when you run work has a dramatic impact.

Norway to Poland is up to 40× difference in carbon intensity. Scotland to London is 6× on the same grid. Region selection is a real lever — not a theoretical one.

◆
Use unit metrics to track intensity, not just totals.

Total emissions are the rear-view mirror. Carbon per transaction, per VM-hour, per device tells you whether what you're doing is getting more or less efficient.

◆
A directional, honest baseline beats a precise, shaky one.

Document confidence levels, build a clear improvement plan, and avoid false precision at the expense of operational relevance.

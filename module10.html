Module 10  ·  Track 3 — Leadership and Strategy

The Business Case: Value,
Risk and Strategic Opportunity

The most common gap in sustainable IT programmes is the absence of a proper business case. The financial, risk, and strategic value case often goes unbuilt — which means the programme competes for investment without the argument that will actually win the room.

Duration24–28 minutes
TrackLeadership and Strategy

What you will take from this module

  • 01Understand the three-dimension business case — financial, risk, and non-financial — and why all three must be present
  • 02Know how to lead with waste reduction as the financial entry point that funds the programme
  • 03Understand how to quantify regulatory and reputational risk exposure as the risk argument
  • 04Know the non-financial value levers — talent, investor access, commercial requirements
  • 05Understand how to frame the case for different audiences: CFO, board, business units

Section 1

The three-dimension business case.

Financial value alone underplays the risk exposure. Risk management alone sounds defensive. Non-financial value alone lacks the operational grounding that wins resource allocation decisions. All three dimensions must be present.

Dimension 1 — The money argument

Financial value

In many organisations the initial waste reduction exercise produces savings large enough to fund the programme. Start there.

  • Waste reduction — often funds the programme itself
  • Improved total cost of ownership: refresh cycles, circular procurement, data minimisation, app rationalisation
  • Access to green financing: bonds, sustainability-linked loans, ESG capital
  • Revenue: sustainability criteria in enterprise and public sector procurement — growing competitive dimension
Dimension 2 — The risk argument

Risk management

Boards respond to quantified risk. Frame regulatory exposure as a time-bounded compliance gap with a penalty dimension.

  • Regulatory risk: CSRD, EU Taxonomy, and equivalent frameworks — compliance gaps carry penalties and reputational exposure
  • Reputational risk: greenwashing claims attract regulatory challenge and legal exposure in multiple jurisdictions
  • Supply chain risk: concentration, geopolitical disruption, material scarcity in technology supply chains
  • Physical climate risk: data centres in exposed locations face operational disruption from extreme weather and water stress
Dimension 3 — The strategic argument

Non-financial value

Boards often find the non-financial case most compelling. Connect it to specific strategic priorities.

  • Talent: sustainability performance influences technology professionals' decisions — particularly earlier in careers
  • Employee engagement: credible programmes generate engagement; performative ones generate cynicism
  • Investor and ESG screening: institutional capital increasingly requires sustainability performance, affecting cost of capital
  • Customer requirements: procurement criteria demand evidence; a cleaner estate is also faster to run

Section 2

Leading with waste reduction.

The financial case for GreenOps is often strongest at the start — before the programme requires significant investment. Waste elimination produces immediate returns and funds what comes next.

£2–8k
per VM per year in cloud run cost — idle instances are the fastest-returning target
£40–120k
per application per year in run and licence cost — portfolio rationalisation is the highest-leverage action
10–20%
of app spend typically recoverable through portfolio rationalisation alone

The self-funding programme

The financial return from Phase 1 waste elimination — idle compute, zombie apps, storage rationalisation — typically covers the cost of the measurement and governance infrastructure needed for subsequent phases. This is the argument that gets the first budget approved without requiring a leap of faith.

Knowledge Check · Module 10 · Q1

A sustainability director is presenting the business case for a GreenOps investment programme to the CFO. The CFO says: "I understand the sustainability rationale but I need to see financial returns before approving budget." Which response is most likely to be effective?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

The CFO's request for financial returns is not an obstacle — it is a legitimate requirement that a well-constructed GreenOps business case can satisfy directly. Phase 1 waste elimination (idle compute, zombie applications, dark data) produces direct cost savings. Regulatory compliance gaps create quantifiable penalty exposure. Sustainability-linked loan mechanisms can improve financing terms.

Option A misframes the conversation — regulatory compliance is part of the argument, but leading with it when the CFO has explicitly asked for financial returns is unlikely to move the conversation forward. The strongest response combines all three financial dimensions simultaneously.

⏸ Pause & Reflect

Take 5–10 minutes. Specificity matters more than completeness.

1Of the three financial value dimensions — waste reduction, improved TCO, green financing, and revenue — which is most relevant to your organisation right now? Do you have the data to make that argument in a budget conversation?
2Think about the four risk dimensions: regulatory, reputational, supply chain, and physical climate. Which represents the most material unquantified exposure in your organisation today?
3Consider your most important non-financial value dimension. Is your current programme credible enough to support that claim? Or is there a gap between the claim and the evidence?

Key Takeaways

◆
Build the full three-dimension case — financial, risk, and non-financial.

Financial alone is insufficient. Risk alone sounds defensive. Non-financial alone lacks operational grounding. All three dimensions must be present for a case that survives scrutiny from finance, risk, and the board simultaneously.

◆
Lead with waste reduction — it often funds the programme.

Idle compute, over-provisioned storage, unnecessary licences, premature refresh: these produce direct cost savings. Starting here creates early wins and builds credibility.

◆
Quantify risk exposure — especially regulatory and reputational.

Boards respond to quantified risk. Frame regulatory exposure as a time-bounded compliance gap with a penalty dimension.

◆
Translate the case for each audience.

Engineers: operational excellence. Finance: cost and risk discipline. Board: strategic and regulatory imperative. The substance doesn't change. The framing must.

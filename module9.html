Section 1

The regulatory landscape has shifted — and it will keep shifting.

Sustainability is no longer voluntary for enterprise technology organisations. A sequence of interconnected frameworks now requires measurement, disclosure, and reduction — with auditable evidence. Technology leaders need to understand what applies, when, and what it specifically requires of IT.

Regulatory milestones and their IT implications — click any event
The regulatory timeline is accelerating

Paris Agreement commitments created science-based targets. Science-based targets created Scope 3 obligations. Scope 3 obligations created supply chain pressure. CSRD turned that pressure into a legal requirement with audit obligations. The direction of travel is clear and it is not reversing. Technology estates that are not measured cannot be reported, and cannot be improved.

Section 2

CSRD double materiality — what it means for IT.

CSRD introduces double materiality — the requirement to assess both how sustainability topics affect the organisation financially, and how the organisation's activities impact the environment and society. For IT, both directions are material.

Financial materiality → IT as a risk

How sustainability issues affect the organisation's financial position and performance. For IT: energy costs, data centre power availability constraints, hardware supply chain risk from climate events, and regulatory compliance cost.

  • →Rising energy costs as carbon pricing extends
  • →Data centre power availability as a bottleneck in constrained markets
  • →Hardware supply chain disruption from rare earth constraints
  • →Regulatory non-compliance cost and legal exposure
Impact materiality → IT as a contributor

How the organisation's activities impact the environment and society. For IT: energy consumption, carbon emissions, water use in cooling, e-waste generation, and supply chain social impacts from hardware manufacture.

  • →Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions from IT operations and supply chain
  • →Water consumption from data centre cooling
  • →E-waste from hardware disposal and premature refresh cycles
  • →Social impacts in hardware manufacturing supply chains
What CSRD specifically requires of IT measurement

Energy consumption data with defined boundaries. GHG emissions covering Scope 1, 2, and material Scope 3 — including cloud and hardware supply chain. Water use where material. E-waste data. All with sufficient quality for third-party assurance. Spend-based proxies for Scope 3 will not meet the assurance standard for most in-scope organisations at the compliance date.

Section 3

Sustainable finance as a practical lever.

Green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, and ESG-screened capital are becoming practical instruments — not just aspirational tools. Technology investment increasingly intersects with these mechanisms.

Knowledge Check · Module 9 · Q1

An organisation's sustainability report states that its data centre operations are powered by 100% renewable electricity, citing Renewable Energy Certificates. Under CSRD and the GHG Protocol, what is the most precise statement about what this claim represents?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

Under the GHG Protocol, organisations report Scope 2 using both the location-based method (reflecting actual grid carbon intensity) and the market-based method (reflecting contractual instruments like RECs). The REC claim is valid for market-based reporting — but it does not change the physical grid that powers the facility.

CSRD requires both methods to be disclosed. An organisation reporting only the market-based figure, without also disclosing the location-based intensity, is providing an incomplete picture. The location-based figure is the physically honest number — the one GreenOps operational decisions should be based on.

Knowledge Check · Module 9 · Q2

A technology director says: "CSRD is a European regulation so it doesn't apply to us — we're headquartered outside the EU." Under what circumstances might CSRD still be relevant to this organisation?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option B

CSRD directly applies to EU-headquartered organisations and to non-EU companies with significant EU revenue above specified thresholds. But the indirect reach extends further: any organisation that is a material supplier to a CSRD-obligated entity will face sustainability data requests — because those entities are required to cover material Scope 3 emissions, including supply chain.

This supply chain pull effect means the regulatory boundary is effectively wider than the direct applicability of the regulation. Organisations that sell to EU-based enterprises, access EU capital markets, or have significant EU operations should treat CSRD as material to their governance planning.

Key Takeaways

◆
Paris Agreement and SBTi put IT estates inside corporate target boundaries.

For most large enterprises, technology consumption is material enough to sit within the SBTi boundary. Technology leaders are directly on the hook for delivery — not just adjacent to it.

◆
EU Taxonomy determines access to sustainable capital — and creates evidence requirements.

For EU-based or EU-funded organisations, Taxonomy alignment affects green financing access and lending terms.

◆
CSRD requires assurable IT data — spend-based proxies are insufficient.

Double materiality means both financial risk and impact must be reported. Digital footprint data must be capable of audit. Scope 3 must be included where material.

◆
Sustainable finance instruments are becoming practical levers for technology investment.

Green bonds, sustainability-linked loans, and ESG-screened capital are connecting sustainability performance directly to cost of capital.

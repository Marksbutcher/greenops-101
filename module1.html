Section 1

The era of digital abundance is ending.

For two decades IT strategy was built on the assumption that compute, storage, and energy were effectively unlimited. Power availability for data centres is already a bottleneck in London, Dublin, Amsterdam, and Singapore. That assumption is gone.

The foundational premise

Digital sustainability is not about being green as a brand posture. It is about operational excellence under constraint. Every wasted VM, every orphaned terabyte, every oversized AI call is a cost and a footprint simultaneously. The financial case and the sustainability case are the same case.

Section 2

Where IT emissions sit — and what cloud actually changes.

Cloud moves most IT emissions from Scope 1 and 2 into Scope 3. It does not reduce them. For most modern technology estates, Scope 3 is 70–80% of total footprint and the hardest to measure.

The most consequential misconception in enterprise IT sustainability

"We moved to cloud so our environmental impact is minimal. Data centres are someone else's problem now."

Cloud moves emissions from Scope 1 and 2 into Scope 3 — still your footprint under CSRD and SBTi. Cloud providers run on energy with its own carbon intensity by region. A shared responsibility model applies: providers manage the infrastructure, you manage how you use it.

Section 3

The policy landscape has changed.

Sustainability is no longer voluntary for most large organisations. A sequence of interconnected frameworks now requires IT to measure, disclose, and reduce — with auditable evidence, not aspirational statements.

Section 5

The GreenOps action model.

Most organisations that describe themselves as having a sustainability programme are running a reporting function. That is a starting point. The goal is to shift left — from visibility to action to prevention.

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q1

Which of the following best describes the Scope 3 emissions challenge for enterprise IT?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

For most modern technology estates, Scope 3 accounts for 70–80% of total footprint. Yet most organisations have the least visibility into it. Cloud services, SaaS platforms, hardware manufacturing, end-of-life treatment, logistics, and packaging all sit in Scope 3. Moving to cloud doesn't eliminate this footprint — it relocates it from Scope 2 into Scope 3, where it remains your organisation's responsibility under CSRD and SBTi frameworks.

Renewable energy certificates address Scope 2 market-based reporting. They do not reduce the Scope 3 emissions embedded in your hardware supply chain or your cloud provider's infrastructure operations.

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q2

An organisation announces it has "moved to cloud and eliminated its data centre carbon footprint." What is the most accurate assessment of this claim?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

Moving to cloud is one of the most widely held and consequential misconceptions in enterprise IT sustainability. Cloud moves IT emissions from Scope 1 and 2 into Scope 3 — it does not eliminate them. Under CSRD and SBTi, Scope 3 is required where material — and for organisations using cloud at scale, it always is.

Cloud providers run on energy with its own carbon intensity, which varies significantly by region. A shared responsibility model applies: providers manage infrastructure efficiency; the IT organisation is responsible for how it uses that infrastructure and for engaging providers on sustainability performance.

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q3

In the GreenOps action model, which level involves preventing unnecessary demand from materialising in the first place?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

Avoidance is the most mature and impactful level. Rather than measuring waste (Reporting) or reducing it after the fact (Optimisation), Avoidance prevents the demand from occurring — through portfolio rationalisation, shift-left architecture decisions, data minimisation policies, and choosing not to run heavyweight AI where it isn't needed.

Most organisations are stuck at Reporting. The financial and sustainability case is to progress to Optimisation and then into Avoidance — to shift left in the lifecycle where intervention is cheapest and most effective.

Module 1 — Key Takeaways

◆
Digital is physical.

ICT accounts for 2–4% of global GHG emissions, with growing water and hardware footprints. The assumption that digital is inherently clean is wrong.

◆
The policy landscape has changed.

CSRD, EU Taxonomy, TCFD, and science-based targets mean IT sustainability is no longer voluntary. It's a compliance and risk management requirement.

◆
Cloud moves the footprint — it doesn't remove it.

Scope 3 emissions from cloud and supply chain are still your organisation's responsibility. A shared responsibility model applies.

◆
Waste is everywhere — and it's costing you twice.

Idle compute, storage bloat, zombie apps, fixed-cycle device refresh, and ungoverned AI consumption are all recoverable financially and environmentally.

◆
Start with waste, not reports.

Reporting / Optimisation / Avoidance. Most organisations are stuck at reporting. The commercial and sustainability case is to go further.

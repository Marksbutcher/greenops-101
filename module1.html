Section 1

The era of digital abundance is ending.

For two decades, IT strategy was built on unlimited storage, elastic compute, and energy abstracted away behind a data centre boundary. That assumption is changing.

The global digital footprint is no longer small enough to ignore. ICT now accounts for 2–4% of global greenhouse gas emissions — comparable to aviation. The water and materials embedded in that infrastructure are orders of magnitude larger than most technology leaders appreciate. And the assumption that moving to cloud makes these someone else's problem is one of the most widely held and consequential misconceptions in enterprise IT.

Why IT can't claim to be a minor contributor

The most common defence — "our IT estate is too small to matter" — fails at enterprise scale. A large bank, retailer, or government body running hundreds of thousands of devices, multiple data centres, and significant cloud estate has a material footprint. The claim doesn't survive scrutiny once someone builds the baseline.

The handprint story

Scope 1 — Direct emissions. Fuel used in backup generators, on-site combustion, and refrigerants in cooling systems. For most IT organisations operating in modern facilities, this is the narrowest category. If you operate your own data centres with diesel backup power and use refrigerant-based cooling, this is where those emissions land.

Diesel generators On-site combustion Refrigerants (cooling) Emergency backup systems

Scope 2 — Indirect energy. Electricity used by data centres you operate, offices, network equipment, and end-user devices in active use. If you run your own data centres, this is significant. Scope 2 has two reporting methods — location-based (actual grid intensity) and market-based (contractual instruments like RECs). Both have legitimate uses, but they measure different things.

Data centre electricity Office buildings Network equipment End-user devices in use

Scope 3 — Value chain emissions. Cloud services, SaaS, managed services, hardware manufacturing, end-of-life treatment, logistics, and packaging. For most modern technology estates, this is the dominant category — often 70–80% of total footprint. It is also the hardest to measure, requiring supplier data and lifecycle assessments rather than direct metering.

The cloud misconception: Moving to cloud does not eliminate IT's environmental footprint — it moves it. Cloud consumption shifts most IT emissions from Scope 1 and 2 into Scope 3. Under CSRD and SBTi, Scope 3 is required where material — and for IT it almost always is. A shared responsibility model applies: providers manage infrastructure; you manage how you use it.

Cloud services SaaS platforms Hardware manufacturing End-of-life treatment Logistics and packaging

Scope 4 — Avoided emissions (handprint). How technology enables carbon reductions elsewhere — the positive side of the ledger. A logistics platform that eliminates unnecessary transport. A smart building system that reduces energy demand. Real value — but not a substitute for managing your own footprint first. The handprint story only lands credibly once footprint discipline is in place.

Avoided transport (logistics platforms) Building energy reduction Remote work substitution Precision agriculture systems

Section 3

The policy landscape has changed.

Sustainability is no longer voluntary. A series of interconnected regulatory frameworks now reshape what technology organisations must disclose, measure, and demonstrate.

Corporate

Science-based targets now commonly cover Scope 3

Cloud consumption and supply chain emissions are inside the SBTi boundary for most large enterprises. IT's footprint is material — and the corporate sustainability team knows it.

Legal exposure

Greenwashing claims face legal challenge

Unsupported sustainability claims — including those made by IT teams — create legal liability in multiple jurisdictions. Non-compliance is not just reputational risk.

Disclosure

CSRD, EU Taxonomy and TCFD obligations

Mandatory disclosure frameworks are expanding. For entities in scope, IT measurement quality directly affects the credibility of corporate reporting. Weak IT data is a weak annual report.

Supply chain

ESG obligations extend to technology vendors

Supplier pressure is flowing down the chain. Your IT vendors are increasingly being asked for the same evidence you are asking them to provide. The ask will return to you.

Five categories of digital waste.

If reporting is the wrong place to start — and it usually is — waste is the right one. It's where you build credibility fast, because outcomes are tangible. Every category below is simultaneously a cost problem and a sustainability problem.

The operational case for GreenOps

"If you're consuming compute, storage, or network that creates no value, you're paying for it twice: once on the invoice, and once in the carbon account."

GreenOps is a discipline that engineers, finance, procurement, and leadership should all care about equally.

Section 5

The GreenOps action model.

Most organisations that describe themselves as having a sustainability programme are, on closer inspection, running a reporting function. That is a starting point. It is not a destination.

1
Reporting Tells you what happened. The rear-view mirror. ▼

Necessary for disclosure, compliance, and setting baselines. Not sufficient for operational improvement. Most organisations are at this level — and don't always know it.

If this is all you have, you have a communications function, not a sustainability programme. The output is data about the past. The question it cannot answer is: what are we doing about it?

Symptom: metrics exist, dashboards exist, annual reports published — but operational decisions are unchanged
2
Optimisation Doing the same thing with less. ▼

Rightsizing, improving utilisation, reducing facility overhead, extending device lifespans. This is where most operational GreenOps effort sits — and where the most immediate wins are found.

Critical caveat: optimisation does not guarantee absolute reduction. A business growing at 20% per year while improving efficiency at 10% is still growing its footprint. This is where the rebound effect lives (covered in Module 5).

Symptom: efficiency metrics improve, but total emissions stay flat or rise as demand grows alongside
3
Avoidance Designing demand out before it exists. ▼

Consolidating duplicate platforms. Retiring unused applications. Designing services that require less compute. The highest-leverage decisions live here — because you are preventing waste, not managing it after the fact.

This is shift-left in practice. It requires earlier intervention than most governance models currently allow. Portfolio decisions, architecture choices, and data minimisation policies are all avoidance mechanisms.

Most organisations are at Level 1. The goal is to know your position honestly, and identify what is blocking the next step.

Where is your organisation right now?

Honest self-placement is more valuable than optimistic placement. Most organisations sit at Level 1 or between 1 and 2. That is normal. The question is: what is blocking the next step?

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q1

Which of the following best describes the Scope 3 emissions challenge for enterprise IT?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

For most modern technology estates, Scope 3 accounts for 70–80% of total footprint. Yet most organisations have the least visibility into it. Cloud services, SaaS platforms, hardware manufacturing, end-of-life treatment, logistics, and packaging all sit in Scope 3. Moving to cloud doesn't eliminate this footprint — it relocates it from Scope 2 into Scope 3, where it remains your organisation's responsibility under CSRD and SBTi frameworks.

Renewable energy certificates address Scope 2 market-based reporting. They do not reduce the Scope 3 emissions embedded in your hardware supply chain or your cloud provider's infrastructure operations.

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q2

An organisation announces it has "moved to cloud and eliminated its data centre carbon footprint." What is the most accurate assessment of this claim?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

Moving to cloud is one of the most widely held and consequential misconceptions in enterprise IT sustainability. Cloud moves IT emissions from Scope 1 and 2 into Scope 3 — it does not eliminate them. Under CSRD and SBTi, Scope 3 is required where material — and for organisations using cloud at scale, it always is.

Cloud providers run on energy with its own carbon intensity, which varies significantly by region. A shared responsibility model applies: providers manage infrastructure efficiency; the IT organisation is responsible for how it uses that infrastructure and for engaging providers on sustainability performance.

Knowledge Check · Module 1 · Q3

In the GreenOps action model, which level involves preventing unnecessary demand from materialising in the first place?

Select an answer to reveal the explanation.

✓ Correct: Option C

Avoidance is the most mature and impactful level. Rather than measuring waste (Reporting) or reducing it after the fact (Optimisation), Avoidance prevents the demand from occurring — through portfolio rationalisation, shift-left architecture decisions, data minimisation policies, and choosing not to run heavyweight AI where it isn't needed.

Most organisations are stuck at Reporting. The financial and sustainability case is to progress to Optimisation and then into Avoidance — to shift left in the lifecycle where intervention is cheapest and most effective.

Module 1 — Key Takeaways

◆
Digital is physical.

ICT accounts for 2–4% of global GHG emissions, with growing water and hardware footprints. The assumption that digital is inherently clean is wrong.

◆
The policy landscape has changed.

CSRD, EU Taxonomy, TCFD, and science-based targets mean IT sustainability is no longer voluntary. It's a compliance and risk management requirement.

◆
Cloud moves the footprint — it doesn't remove it.

Scope 3 emissions from cloud and supply chain are still your organisation's responsibility. A shared responsibility model applies.

◆
Waste is everywhere — and it's costing you twice.

Idle compute, storage bloat, zombie apps, fixed-cycle device refresh, and ungoverned AI consumption are all recoverable financially and environmentally.

◆
Start with waste, not reports.

Reporting / Optimisation / Avoidance. Most organisations are stuck at reporting. The commercial and sustainability case is to go further.
